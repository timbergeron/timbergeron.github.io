Learn How to Bunnyhop in Quake

This page teaches the core ideas behind Quake-style bunnyhopping using two great video references. The main idea is simple: you do not gain speed by mindlessly holding forward in a straight line. You gain speed from turning, strafing, timing your jumps, and controlling your movement in the air.

Quick learner 80/20: get most of the gains fast

If you only want the handful of ideas that produce most of the improvement, focus on these first. This is the highest-return path for building usable bunnyhop speed without getting lost in every edge case.

The 20% that gives most of the results

  1. Stop trying to run straight. Speed comes from turning plus strafing, not from holding forward and hoping.
  2. Learn one clean circle jump. Build speed on the ground with a smooth turn, then jump before friction kills it.
  3. In the air, favor strafe + mouse turn. Let the movement arc happen instead of forcing normal steering.
  4. Jump again quickly on landing. Time on the ground is where speed dies.
  5. Practice on a big open map. Open space makes it easier to feel the rhythm before adding tight maneuvering.

What to actually do for your first gains

  • Start from standstill or simple walking speed instead of from a perfect high-speed pre-strafe.
  • Practice chaining just 2 to 4 clean jumps before trying long sequences.
  • Use one strafe direction at first until the turning feel makes sense.
  • Think “swing around the pole” instead of “steer like a car.”
  • Only later add direction switching, wallhugging, and power bunnyhopping.
Best beginner drill: do a modest circle jump, hold a single strafe direction in the air, turn smoothly into that direction, and focus on landing into the next jump quickly. That one drill teaches the core of Quake movement faster than almost anything else.

What bunnyhopping actually is

Bunnyhopping is the combination of an initial speed-building move on the ground, a well-timed jump, and repeated air control so you keep that speed instead of letting friction destroy it. In Quake and Quake-like movement systems, this creates movement that feels more like racing than ordinary walking.

1. Build speed with a turn

Turning while using the correct movement input increases your speed more effectively than just moving straight forward.

2. Jump before friction wins

Ground friction pulls your speed back down. Jumping at the right moment lets you carry your built-up speed into the air.

3. Keep steering in the air

Once airborne, your strafe keys and mouse movement let you curve smoothly and preserve momentum across repeated jumps.

The simplest mental model: “grab the pole”

One of the best ideas from the turning-behavior video is to imagine your movement key as a hand grabbing a pole. If you press right strafe, imagine there is a pole on your right and you are swinging around it. If you press left, you are orbiting a pole on your left. This makes turning behavior much easier to feel and predict.

How to visualize it

  • D / right strafe = grab a pole on your right and swing around it.
  • A / left strafe = grab a pole on your left and swing around it.
  • Diagonal inputs are just diagonal “hands” grabbing a diagonal pole.
  • The direction of your view changes how that arc feels, but the same principle still applies.

Why this helps

  • It stops you from thinking movement is just “hold forward and steer.”
  • It helps explain why new players often fail when they try to force intuitive car-like steering.
  • It makes sideways and diagonal movement feel natural instead of weird.
  • It teaches maneuvering, not just raw speed.
Beginner mistake: holding forward and trying to turn normally in the air often feels wrong because Quake-style air movement is not ordinary steering. Think in arcs, not straight lines.

Step-by-step: your first bunnyhop

Use this as your beginner sequence. The goal is not perfect speed immediately. The goal is to understand the rhythm: strafe, turn, jump, land cleanly, and repeat.

01

Start moving and build a small arc

Begin with a short run-up. Instead of only pushing straight forward, start turning gently while using an appropriate strafe input. You are trying to build speed through a curve, not a line.

02

Jump as you carry that speed

The jump transfers your speed from the ground into the air before friction drags you back toward normal movement speed.

03

Release the “walk straight” mindset

In the air, think about curving with your mouse and strafe keys instead of trying to push straight forward. The goal is smooth control, not jerky correction.

04

Land and jump again quickly

Repeated jumps keep your speed alive. If you spend too long on the ground, friction eats your momentum.

05

Use left and right arcs rhythmically

Bunnyhopping often becomes a smooth left-right pattern. Each jump is an opportunity to keep curving and preserving speed.

What the videos say matters most

From the turning behavior video

  • Different movement keys create different turning centers.
  • You can bunnyhop sideways, diagonally, or forward depending on the situation.
  • Circle jumps and pre-strafes are based on the same turning principle.

From the beginner speedrunning guide

  • Bunnyhopping combines strafe jump, repeated jumps, and air control.
  • It is not only about maximum speed; it is also about precise maneuvering.
  • Good runners adjust angle and speed based on the geometry of the room.
  • Related movement tools include wall hugging, ground strafing, zigzagging, and power bunnyhopping.

Practice plan

Train one skill at a time. Do not try to learn every advanced movement option at once.

Stage Focus What to feel for
1 Ground turning Notice that curved movement builds speed better than simply running straight.
2 Single strafe jump Feel the moment where the jump preserves the speed you just built.
3 Two to three chained jumps Work on smooth landings and immediate re-jumps without panic-correcting.
4 Air control Use mouse turning plus strafe input to create a clean arc in the air.
5 Maneuvering Practice not just speed, but steering through spaces without hitting walls or ceilings.

Common beginner mistakes

Holding forward like it is normal movement

That often makes bunnyhopping feel clumsy. Focus on the arc created by strafe input and mouse turn.

Landing too long on the ground

Ground friction is the enemy. The longer you stay grounded, the more speed you lose.

Only thinking about speed

Bunnyhopping is also for positioning and maneuvering. The fastest path is not always a straight one.

Open Quake Movement Speed Training

Based on shalrathy’s training page, here are the biggest takeaways that fit this tutorial.

  • Use an open training map. 100m.bsp is ideal because it is bright, open, and easy for measuring progress.
  • Bunnyhopping in NetQuake and QuakeWorld is different. QuakeWorld removes the ground-friction penalty between bunnyhops, which makes sustained speed much easier and raises the speed ceiling dramatically.
  • Start simple. The page recommends beginning from standstill or walking speed so you can clearly feel when your air turning is helping, rather than starting from a fast circle jump and immediately needing near-perfect execution.
  • Train with instrumentation if your engine supports it. Tools like scr_speed, jump info, speed history, and angle plots make it much easier to see whether your turning angle is helping or hurting.
  • Power bunnyhopping is mostly about reducing landing losses. It is not magic free speed; it is largely about minimizing the speed destroyed by the ground frame between jumps.
  • Muscle memory matters. The page repeatedly emphasizes that bunnyhopping and power bunnyhopping require many fast, precise actions, so repetition is the only real path to consistency.

Best beginner takeaway

Start on an open map, begin from low speed, and watch whether each jump is actually building speed. Low-speed practice teaches the movement pattern much faster than jumping straight into max-speed attempts.

Best intermediate takeaway

Learn the difference between circle-jumping, regular bunnyhopping, and power bunnyhopping. They are related, but each has a different purpose and different optimal angles.

Best advanced takeaway

At higher speeds the “correct” view angle window gets narrower, which is why great runs look so smooth but are actually very unforgiving. Tiny mistakes cost a lot more once you are already moving fast.

Source-checked bunnyhop math

These numbers are taken from the actual movement code in QSS-M and ezQuake, not from second-hand movement folklore. Both engines use the same air-acceleration geometry, but they differ sharply on whether landing-frame friction can be skipped.

The air-angle math both codebases share

In both sources, air acceleration only works while the projection of your speed onto your intended movement direction stays below 30. If s is horizontal speed and δ is the angle between your current velocity and wishdir, the useful part is:

gain starts when s * cos(δ) < 30 δ_min = arccos(30 / s)
default Quake accel step = 10 * 320 * dt at normal frame times, best wishdir is almost 90° to velocity usable angle window near that best line = 90° - δ_min = arcsin(30 / s)

Where QSS-M and ezQuake split

  • ezQuake / QuakeWorld: PM_CheckJump() runs before PM_Friction(), so a same-frame landing jump skips ground friction.
  • QSS-M / NetQuake-style: in the NQ bunnyfriction path, PM_Friction() runs first and PM_CheckJump() runs after movement, so each chained hop pays one grounded frame.
  • Manual timing: both codepaths keep a jump-held anti-pogo timer for roughly 50 ms, so manual hopping still needs a release and repress unless autobunny is enabled.

QSS-M defaults also change the forward+strafe angle: cl_forwardspeed 200 and cl_sidespeed 350 point the move vector 60.26° off the camera, while ezQuake’s 400 / 400 defaults point it 45° off the camera.

The real precision requirement is simple to state and hard to execute: as your speed rises, the correct line collapses into a tiny band around perpendicular. At 500 u/s the usable band is about 3.44°. At 1000 u/s it shrinks to about 1.72°.

Recommended training progression

  1. Learn speed turning on the ground.
  2. Add a consistent circle jump.
  3. Chain a few bunnyhops with one strafe direction.
  4. Learn left-right switching to move forward cleanly.
  5. Use open-map drills on 100m.bsp or similar maps.
  6. Only then start experimenting with wallhugging, zigzagging, and power bunnyhopping.

Quick recap

To bunnyhop well in Quake, think in curves. Build speed by turning, jump before ground friction slows you down, and keep steering in the air with your mouse and strafe keys. Use the “grab the pole” idea to understand how turning works, and remember that bunnyhopping is not just about going fast — it is about moving exactly where you want with momentum.
